t-test – Independent Samples

One of the most well-known hypothesis tests, the t-test is used to assess the equality of two population statistics (typically means) based on samples from those populations. More specifically, this test is used when:

  1. You have two separate sets of independent and identically distributed samples. E.g. a treatment group and a control group, not a before and after treatment comparison ("paired samples").
  2. You want to assess if a treatment led to some measureable difference in the groups (e.g. giving the treatment group a low fat diet led to a reduction in average weight).
  3. The sample means (not neccessarily the underlying data) are independent and normally distributed (see Central Limit Theorem).
  4. The sample variance is χ2 distributed, and the sample mean and sample variance are statistically independent.See note 2
  5. You do not know the standard deviation of the underlying populations. If you do, you can instead use a z-test.

If this is the type of test you are conducting, or have already completed, you can use this calculator to:

  1. Estimate how large a sample you will need to detect an expected effect size (at a given power level).
  2. Estimate the power of a planned study based on expected sample and effect sizes. You cannot calculate the power of a completed study based on the observed effect size.
  3. Estimate the smallest effect size you can detect for a given power level and sample size.
  4. Calculate the t-statistic and/or p-value of a completed test.

Note 1: This calculator does not require the groups to have equal variance as it uses the Welch's unequal variances t-test formulation by default – and you probably should too.

Note 2: This assumption holds if the underlying data are normally distributed, but not neccessarily if you are relying on the central limit theorem for normally distributed sample means.

Key Terms

  • Significance Level (α): In order to reject the null hypothesis (H0: θ = 0; where θ = μ1 - μ2), we must observe a difference in sample means that falls in the α most extreme differences in sample means that we would expect to see if the null hypothesis is true.
  • Statistical Power (1 - β): Assuming the alternate hypothesis is true (HA: θ ≠ 0), repeated sampling would cause us to correctly reject the null hypothesis at a rate of 1 - β. This equates to a false negative (Type II error) rate of β.
  • Effect Size (Cohen's d): A standardized measure of the difference in the means (can be sample or population means depending on the context). The difference in means is divided by the pooled standard deviation of the two samples/populations to make the number comparable across different experiments.
