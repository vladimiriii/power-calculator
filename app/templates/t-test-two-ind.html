Student's t-test – Two Independent Samples

Student's t-test for two independent samples is a test that is used when you have:

  1. Two independent and identically distributed samples (e.g. a treatment group and a control group, not the same group before and after some treatment).
  2. You want to assess if a treatment led to some measureable difference in the groups (e.g. giving the treatment group a low fat diet led to a reduction in average weight).
  3. You can reasonably assume (or you have confirmed) that the sample means (not the underlying datapoints themselves) are normally distributed (see Central Limit Theorem).
  4. You do not know the standard deviation of the underlying populations.

Understanding

  • Significance Level (α): In order to reject the null hypothesis (H0: θ = 0; where θ = |μ1 - μ2|), we must observe a difference in sample means that falls in the α most extreme differences in sample means that we would expect to see if we repeatedly drew samples from the same distribution. Values in these regions would lead to a Type I error and are highlighted in red in the chart below.
  • Statistical Power (1 - β): Assuming the alternate hypothesis is true (HA: θ = x; where x is the expected difference in population means), repeated sampling would cause us to correctly reject the null hypothesis at a rate of 1 - β. This equates to a false negative (Type II error) rate of β. The sample mean differences that would lead to a Type II error are highlighted in blue in the chart below.
  • Effect Size (Cohen's d): A standardized measure of the difference in the underlying population means, or the size of the true effect of the treatment. The difference in means is standardized by dividing by the standard deviation.
